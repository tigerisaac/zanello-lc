# Log-concavity of level Hilbert functions of codimension 3 and type 2

**Theorem.** Let \(k\) be a field and let \(A\) be a standard graded artinian level \(k\)-algebra of codimension 3 and Cohen–Macaulay type 2. Then the Hilbert function \(h(A)\) is log-concave.

### Conventions

- \(R = k[x_1,x_2,x_3]\), with maximal homogeneous ideal \(\mathfrak{m} = (x_1,x_2,x_3)\).
- \(N_j = \binom{j+2}{2}\) for \(j \ge 0\), and \(N_j = 0\) for \(j < 0\). Note \(\Delta N_j = N_j - N_{j-1} = j+1\) for \(j \ge 0\), and \(\Delta^2 N_j = 1\) for \(j \ge 0\).
- A finite sequence of nonnegative reals \((a_i)\) is **log-concave** if \(a_{i-1}a_{i+1} \le a_i^2\) for every interior index \(i\).
- Write \(e\) for the socle degree of \(A\) and
  \[
  h = h(A) = (h_0,\ldots,h_e) = (1,3,h_2,\ldots,h_e = 2).
  \]
- Without loss of generality \(I := \ker(R \to A)\) contains no nonzero linear form (else pass to a polynomial ring in fewer variables). Thus the embedding dimension of \(A\) is exactly 3.
- \(K := \operatorname{Frac}(R)\).

The argument is characteristic-free.

---

## 1. Dual module and resolution

Let \(s := e+3\). Define the reindexed graded Matlis dual by
\[
M_d := \operatorname{Hom}_k(A_{e-d},\,k), \qquad d \in \mathbb{Z}.
\]
Then \(M\) is a finitely generated graded \(R\)-module with Hilbert function
\[
g_d := \dim_k M_d = h_{e-d},
\]
supported in degrees \(0,\ldots,e\). Because \(A\) is level of type 2 with socle concentrated in degree \(e\):

- \(M\) is generated by two elements in degree \(0\);
- \(\operatorname{Soc}(M)\) is one-dimensional and concentrated in degree \(e\).

(Indeed, generators of \(M\) correspond to a basis of \(A_e = \operatorname{Soc}(A)\), and \(\operatorname{Soc}(M)\) corresponds to \(A_0\).)

**Lemma 1 (dual resolution).**
Let
\[
0 \to G_3 \to G_2 \to G_1 \to R \to A \to 0
\]
be the minimal graded free resolution of \(A\). Then \(G_3 \cong R(-s)^{\oplus 2}\), and applying \(\operatorname{Hom}_R(-,R(-s))\) yields a minimal graded free resolution
\begin{equation}
0 \longrightarrow R(-s) \longrightarrow F_2 \longrightarrow F_1 \longrightarrow R^{2} \longrightarrow M \longrightarrow 0. \tag{C}
\end{equation}
Writing \(F_1 = \bigoplus_j R(-j)^{p_j}\) and \(F_2 = \bigoplus_j R(-j)^{q_j}\), the summands of \(F_2\) are in bijection with the minimal homogeneous generators of \(I\): a generator of degree \(\delta\) dualizes to a summand \(R(-(s-\delta))\) of \(F_2\).

*Proof.* Since \(A\) is artinian of grade 3, the Auslander–Buchsbaum formula gives \(\operatorname{pd}_R A = 3\). For artinian (equivalently, finite-length) modules the type equals the last Betti number, so \(\operatorname{rank} G_3 = \operatorname{type}(A) = 2\).

Graded local duality over the polynomial ring (valid over any field) yields a graded isomorphism
\[
\operatorname{Ext}^3_R\bigl(A,\, R(-3)\bigr) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_k(A,k),
\]
where \(\operatorname{Hom}_k(A,k)_j = \operatorname{Hom}_k(A_{-j},k)\). Under the convention \(N(a)_d = N_{a+d}\), the reindexed module satisfies \(M_d = (A^\vee)_{d-e}\), so \(M \cong \operatorname{Hom}_k(A,k)(-e)\). Graded local duality then gives \(A^\vee \cong \operatorname{Ext}^3_R(A,R(-3))\), and therefore
\[
M \cong \operatorname{Ext}^3_R\bigl(A,\, R(-s)\bigr),
\]
with generators in degree \(0\).

Dualizing the resolution of \(A\) by \(\operatorname{Hom}_R(-,R(-s))\) produces an exact complex resolving this Ext module. Concretely,
\[
F_2 = \operatorname{Hom}_R(G_1,R(-s)), \qquad
F_1 = \operatorname{Hom}_R(G_2,R(-s)), \qquad
\operatorname{Hom}_R(G_3,R(-s)) \cong R^{2},
\]
giving (C). Levelness forces both socle generators of \(A\) into degree \(e\), hence both shifts on \(G_3\) equal \(e+3 = s\), i.e. \(G_3 \cong R(-s)^{\oplus 2}\) and \(\operatorname{Hom}_R(G_3,R(-s)) \cong R^{2}\) in degree \(0\).

The map \(G_1 \to R\) is the row of minimal homogeneous generators of \(I\). Dualizing gives the stated dictionary between those generators and the summands of \(F_2\): \(\operatorname{Hom}_R(R(-\delta),R(-s)) \cong R(-(s-\delta))\). Minimality is preserved by duality. \(\square\)

**Lemma 2 (full-support last syzygy).**
In (C), every coordinate of the map \(R(-s) \to F_2\) is nonzero. Consequently, over \(K\), every proper subset of the columns of \(F_2 \to F_1\) is \(K\)-linearly independent.

*Proof.* By Lemma 1, the map \(R(-s) \to F_2\) is the dual of \(G_1 \to R\), whose matrix is the row of minimal homogeneous generators of \(I\). Each minimal generator is a nonzero form, so every coordinate is nonzero. The kernel of \(F_2 \otimes_R K \to F_1 \otimes_R K\) is therefore one-dimensional, spanned by a vector with no zero coordinate. Any proper subset of columns is hence \(K\)-independent. \(\square\)

## 2. Circuit inequality

Fix an integer \(d\) with \(2 \le d \le e\). Set
\[
Q_d := \sum_{j \le d} q_j, \qquad
P_{<d} := \sum_{j < d} p_j, \qquad
r_d := \operatorname{rank}_K\bigl(F_{1,<d} \to R^{2}\bigr),
\]
and let \(\varepsilon_d = 1\) if \(Q_d = \operatorname{rank} F_2\) and \(\varepsilon_d = 0\) otherwise. (Here \(F_{1,<d}\) denotes the sum of summands of \(F_1\) of shift strictly less than \(d\).)

**Lemma 3 (circuit rank).**
The columns of \(F_2\) of shift at most \(d\) have \(K\)-rank \(Q_d - \varepsilon_d\). Moreover
\begin{equation}
Q_d - \varepsilon_d \le P_{<d} - r_d. \tag{1}
\end{equation}

*Proof.* By Lemma 2, those columns are \(K\)-dependent if and only if they comprise all of \(F_2\), in which case the rank drops by exactly one. This gives rank \(Q_d - \varepsilon_d\).

Minimality of (C) forces every entry of \(F_2 \to F_1\) to lie in \(\mathfrak{m}\), so a column of shift \(\le d\) lands only in rows of \(F_1\) of shift \(< d\). The image of the low-shift columns therefore lies in
\[
\ker\bigl(F_{1,<d}\otimes_R K \to K^{2}\bigr),
\]
which has dimension \(P_{<d} - r_d\). Comparing dimensions yields (1). \(\square\)

**Lemma 4 (second differences).**
For \(d < s\),
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{2} g_d := g_d - 2g_{d-1} + g_{d-2}
= 2 + Q_d - P_{<d} - p_d
\le 2 - r_d + \varepsilon_d - p_d. \tag{2}
\end{equation}

*Proof.* From (C), for \(d < s\) (so the last term \(R(-s)\) does not yet contribute),
\[
g_d = 2N_d - \sum_j p_j N_{d-j} + \sum_j q_j N_{d-j}.
\]
Taking second differences and using \(\Delta^{2} N_m = 1\) for \(m \ge 0\) (and \(0\) for \(m < 0\)) produces the identity. The inequality is then (1). \(\square\)

**Lemma 4′ (failed LC forces positive second difference).**
If \(g_{d-2},g_{d-1},g_d > 0\) and \(g_{d-2}\,g_d > g_{d-1}^{2}\), then \(\Delta^{2} g_d \ge 1\).

*Proof.* Ordinary concavity \(g_{d-2}+g_d \le 2g_{d-1}\) implies log-concavity by AM–GM:
\[
\sqrt{g_{d-2}\,g_d} \le \tfrac{g_{d-2}+g_d}{2} \le g_{d-1}.
\]
Thus failure of log-concavity implies failure of ordinary concavity. Since Hilbert-function second differences are integers, \(\Delta^{2} g_d \ge 1\).

(The positivity hypothesis holds for \(0 \le d \le e\) because \(A\) is standard graded artinian of socle degree \(e\).) \(\square\)

## 3. Easy cases

**Lemma 5 (\(\varepsilon_d = 1\)).**
If \(\varepsilon_d = 1\), then \((g_{d-2},g_{d-1},g_d)\) is log-concave.

*Proof.* All summands of \(F_2\) have shift \(\le d\). By Lemma 1 they correspond to minimal generators of \(I\) of degrees \(\ge s-d\), so \(I\) has no nonzero form in degree \(< s-d\). Hence \(A_j \cong R_j\) and \(h_j = N_j\) for all \(j < s-d\).

Set \(m := e-d+2 = s-d-1\). Then \(s-d = m+1\), so \(h_j = N_j\) for all \(j \le m\). In particular
\begin{align*}
g_{d-2} &= h_{e-d+2} = h_m = N_m,\\
g_{d-1} &= h_{e-d+1} = h_{m-1} = N_{m-1},\\
g_d &= h_{e-d} = h_{m-2} = N_{m-2}.
\end{align*}
The sequence \((N_j)_{j \ge 0}\) is log-concave (e.g. by direct computation, or as the Hilbert function of \(R\)), so the triple is log-concave. \(\square\)

**Lemma 6 (cases \(r_d \in \{0,2\}\)).**
Assume \(\varepsilon_d = 0\).

- If \(r_d = 2\), then \(\Delta^{2} g_d \le -p_d \le 0\), so \((g_{d-2},g_{d-1},g_d)\) is ordinarily concave and hence log-concave.
- If \(r_d = 0\), there is no nonzero minimal relation of degree \(< d\), so \(g_t = 2N_t\) for all \(t \le d-1\), while \(g_d \le 2N_d\). Log-concavity of \((N_j)\) yields
  \[
  (2N_{d-2})\,g_d \le (2N_{d-2})(2N_d) \le (2N_{d-1})^{2}.
  \]

*Proof.* The first bullet is (2). For the second: \(\ker(R^{2} \to M)_t = 0\) for \(t < d\), so \(M_t \cong (R^{2})_t\) for \(t \le d-1\). \(\square\)

**Lemma 7 (reduction to rank one).**
Suppose \(\varepsilon_d = 0\) and \((g_{d-2},g_{d-1},g_d)\) fails log-concavity. Then \(r_d = 1\), \(p_d = 0\), and \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\).

*Proof.* Lemma 6 rules out \(r_d \in \{0,2\}\), so \(r_d = 1\). From (2),
\[
\Delta^{2} g_d \le 2 - 1 + 0 - p_d = 1 - p_d.
\]
Lemma 4′ gives \(\Delta^{2} g_d \ge 1\). Since \(p_d \ge 0\), necessarily \(p_d = 0\) and \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\). \(\square\)

## 4. Rank-one factorization

Assume throughout this section that \(\varepsilon_d = 0\), \(r_d = 1\), \(p_d = 0\), and \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\).

**Lemma 8 (primitive equal-degree generator of the relation line).**
The images in \(K^{2}\) of all minimal relation columns of shift \(< d\) span a single \(K\)-line. This line is spanned by a primitive homogeneous vector \(v = (v_1,v_2) \in R^{2}\) whose components are homogeneous of a common degree \(a \ge 0\). The \(R\)-submodule of \(R^{2}\) generated by those columns equals \(vJ\) for a homogeneous ideal \(J \subset R\).

*Proof.* The rank hypothesis \(r_d = 1\) says precisely that the stated images span a one-dimensional \(K\)-subspace \(L \subset K^{2}\).

Each minimal relation of degree \(j\) is a homogeneous map \(R(-j) \to R^{2}\), i.e. a pair of forms of equal degree \(j\). Any two nonzero such pairs lying in \(L\) are \(K\)-multiples of one another, so the ratio of their components (when defined) is a degree-\(0\) element of \(K\). Writing that ratio in lowest terms as \(p/q\) with \(p,q\) homogeneous of the same degree and \(\gcd(p,q)=1\), the primitive vector \(v := (q,p)\) lies in \(R^{2}\), has equal-degree components, and spans \(L\).

Every low-shift relation column is then an \(R\)-multiple of \(v\), and the set of all such multipliers is a homogeneous ideal \(J\). \(\square\)

Because \(p_d = 0\), no relation of degree \(d\) exists, and a relation of degree \(> d\) cannot contribute to \(\ker(R^{2} \to M)\) in degrees \(\le d\). Hence
\begin{equation}
\ker(R^{2} \to M)_{t} = (vJ)_{t} \qquad\text{for all } t \le d. \tag{4}
\end{equation}

Let \(H\) denote the image of \(v\) in \(M_a\).

- If \(H = 0\), then \(v\) itself is a relation of degree \(a < d\). Primitivity and (4) force \(1 \in J\), so \(J = R\). In this case set \(B_{\bullet} := 0\) formally (do not interpret \(B\) as a Gorenstein quotient).
- If \(H \ne 0\), the cyclic submodule \(RH\) has nonzero socle contained in \(\operatorname{Soc}(M)\). Thus that socle is one-dimensional and occurs in degree \(e\), and
  \[
  B := R/\operatorname{Ann}(H)
  \]
  is artinian Gorenstein of embedding dimension at most \(3\) and socle degree \(E = e-a\).

Put \(n := d-a\). For every homogeneous \(c \in R\) of degree at most \(n\), the element \(cv\) has degree \(\le d\), so by (4) and primitivity of \(v\),
\[
cv \in \ker(R^{2} \to M) \iff c \in J \iff cH = 0 \iff c \in \operatorname{Ann}(H).
\]
Thus \(J\) and \(\operatorname{Ann}(H)\) agree in all degrees \(\le n\).

**Lemma 9 (Hilbert formula).**
For \(t \in \{d-2,d-1,d\}\),
\begin{equation}
g_t = 2N_t - N_{t-a} + B_{t-a}, \tag{5}
\end{equation}
where negative indices are read as zero, and \(B_{\bullet} = 0\) if \(H = 0\).

*Proof.* For \(t \le d\), exactness gives \(M_t \cong (R^{2})_t/(vJ)_t\). The map \(J_{t-a} \to (R^{2})_t\), \(c \mapsto cv\), is injective because \(R\) is a domain and \(v \ne 0\), so \(\dim(vJ)_t = \dim J_{t-a}\). Agreement of \(J\) and \(\operatorname{Ann}(H)\) through degree \(n\) yields \(\dim J_{t-a} = N_{t-a} - B_{t-a}\) whenever \(t-a \le n\), i.e. \(t \le d\). \(\square\)

**Lemma 10 (degree bound).**
One has \(g_{d-1} \ge N_{d-1}\) and \(2d \le e+2\). Consequently
\[
2(n-1) = 2d-2a-2 \le e-2a \le e-a = E
\]
when \(H \ne 0\).

*Proof.* From (5),
\[
g_{d-1} = 2N_{d-1} - N_{d-1-a} + B_{d-1-a} \ge N_{d-1},
\]
while Macaulay’s bound (or the surjection \(R \twoheadrightarrow A\)) gives \(g_{d-1} = h_{e-d+1} \le N_{e-d+1}\). Since \((N_j)\) is strictly increasing for \(j \ge 0\), one gets \(d-1 \le e-d+1\), i.e. \(2d \le e+2\). The remaining comparisons are immediate. \(\square\)

**Lemma 11 (first-half increment).**
Let \(D := B_{n-1} - B_{n-2}\) (with \(B_{\bullet} = 0\) if \(H = 0\), and the convention \(B_j = 0\) for \(j < 0\)). Then \(D \ge 0\).

*Proof.* If \(H = 0\) then \(D = 0\). If \(H \ne 0\), Lemma 10 gives \(n-1 \le E/2\). It remains to know that the Hilbert function of an artinian Gorenstein \(k\)-algebra of embedding dimension at most \(3\) is nondecreasing through its first half, over every field. This is recorded as Proposition A below. \(\square\)

**Lemma 12 (margin contradiction).**
Write \(b := g_{d-1}\) and \(x := g_{d-1} - g_{d-2}\). Then
\[
x = d + a + D \ge d, \qquad b \le 2N_{d-1} = d(d+1),
\]
the triple is \((b-x,\, b,\, b+x+1)\), and its log-concavity margin equals
\[
b^{2} - (b-x)(b+x+1) = x(x+1) - b \ge d(d+1) - b \ge 0,
\]
contradicting the assumed failure.

*Proof.* From (5) and \(\Delta N_j = j+1\),
\begin{align*}
x
&= 2\bigl(N_{d-1}-N_{d-2}\bigr) - \bigl(N_{d-1-a}-N_{d-2-a}\bigr) + D \\
&= 2d - \bigl(N_{d-1-a}-N_{d-2-a}\bigr) + D.
\end{align*}
If \(d-1-a \ge 0\) then \(N_{d-1-a}-N_{d-2-a} = d-a\), so \(x = d+a+D\). If \(d-1-a < 0\), necessarily \(a = d\) (so \(n = 0\)) and \(N_{d-1-a}-N_{d-2-a} = 0\), whence again \(x = 2d + D = d+a+D\).

(Note: the subcase \(n = 0\) and \(H \ne 0\) cannot actually arise under \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\), since (5) would force \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 2\); it is included only for formal completeness.)

The bound \(b \le 2N_{d-1}\) is the surjection \(R^{2} \twoheadrightarrow M\). From \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\) one has \(g_d = b+x+1\). The margin identity is elementary, and \(x \ge d \ge 2\) forces \(x(x+1) \ge d(d+1)\). \(\square\)

---

## 5. Conclusion

**Proof of the Theorem.**
Fix \(2 \le d \le e\), and suppose \((g_{d-2},g_{d-1},g_d)\) fails log-concavity. Lemma 5 rules out \(\varepsilon_d = 1\). Lemma 7 then forces \(r_d = 1\), \(p_d = 0\), and \(\Delta^{2} g_d = 1\). Lemmas 8–12 produce a nonnegative log-concavity margin, a contradiction.

Thus \(g\) is log-concave at every index \(1,\ldots,e-1\). Since \(g_d = h_{e-d}\), the same inequalities are exactly the log-concavity inequalities for \(h\). \(\square\)

## Appendix A. First-half nondecrease for Artin Gorenstein algebras of embdim \(\le 3\)

**Proposition A.**
Let \(B\) be a standard graded artinian Gorenstein \(k\)-algebra of embedding dimension \(r \le 3\) and socle degree \(E\). Then \(h(B)_0 \le h(B)_1 \le \cdots \le h(B)_{\lfloor E/2 \rfloor}\). This holds over every field.

*Proof.*
Write \(h = h(B)\).

- If \(r = 0\), then \(B \cong k\) and \(E = 0\); there is nothing to prove.
- If \(r = 1\), then \(B \cong k[x]/(x^{E+1})\) and \(h = (1,1,\ldots,1)\), which is nondecreasing.
- If \(r = 2\), Gorenstein \(h\)-vectors are SI-sequences (Macaulay; see e.g. Stanley, *Hilbert functions of graded algebras*, Adv. Math. 28 (1978), or the elementary account in Zanello’s note on Stanley’s theorem). In particular the first half is differentiable, hence (because the first difference of an \(O\)-sequence is a sequence of nonnegative integers) nondecreasing.
- If \(r = 3\), the same conclusion is Stanley’s theorem: \(h\) is an SI-sequence
  ([Stanley, Adv. Math. 28 (1978), Theorem 4.2](https://doi.org/10.1016/0001-8708(78)90045-2);
  elementary characteristic-free proof in Zanello, *Stanley’s theorem on codimension 3 Gorenstein \(h\)-vectors*). Differentiability of the first half again yields nondecrease.

In all cases, \(h_i - h_{i-1} \ge 0\) for \(1 \le i \le \lfloor E/2 \rfloor\). \(\square\)
